Is the Glock 26/27 a better J-frame

06-06-2013

Is the Glock G26/27 a better J-frame than the quintessential 5 shot snub nosed revolver from the likes of Smith and Wesson, Ruger, Taurus and Charter Arms? The Glock is certainly easier to reload; being magazine feed and similarly sized to the 2 inch barreled revolver (clearly shown in the pictures above), but is that enough to make it better? The Glock is 26.32 oz. loaded (with 11 rounds of 9mm 124gr) and the air-weight J-frame loaded (with 5 of 38 special 135gr) is 17.5 oz. almost 9 ounces lighter, or a little more than a half a pound. If your an optimist, you can bring a 5 round reload at the additional cost of 2.5 oz for your revolver, but understand reloads are painfully slow, which will be a very bad thing on the 2 way range and moving to cover or getting “out of dodge” better be on the top of your list things to do pronto. But, how often does a private citizen need a gun, and then needing it, how often does it go more then a couple of shots? See my previous post on “Playing the odds with the J-Frame”. This weight difference in the front pocket is not to be underestimated. As a front pocket gun, the J-frame, with it’s shape make it much quicker and surer out of the pocket then the brick like “Block”, I mean Glock. The “Hammerless” revolver, has very little metal above the grip help prevent it from snagging on your pants pocket when you pull it verse the back of the Glock slide. To carry a loaded Glock, without it’s trigger guard covered is the definition of insanity, but the J-frame with it’s much longer and heavier trigger pull it much safer, as long as it’s the only resident of that pocket (sans keys, pens or pocket knives). In a front coat pocket, the J-frame can come into it’s own, firing it’s entire payload through the pocket with no fear of jamming which is a problem with any semi-auto pistol and having your hand on it while walking through a dark parking lot is comforting. Carrying inside the waist belt (IWB) on a Barami Hip Grip or Clip Draw is a convent and safe to carry without a holster and your little J-frame it’s more organic shape helps with concealment even though the cylinder is slightly wider than the 1 inch width of the Glock slide. If you”Mexican Carry” a Glock, I strongly suggest you have the trigger covered with something like the VanGuard. Any firearm you carry for self protection should be ready to fire when needed, so carrying your Glock “without one in the pipe” is just not a viable option in my point of view. You may be fending off an attack, injured or moving loved ones out of the way, and will only have one hand available to operate your handgun. The Glock G26/27 is a vastly better fighting tool, but requires more thought and attention. All semi-automatic pistols have a more complicated manual of arms, the Glock being about the simplest possible, but still more complicated than the ancient revolver, without the need for any “rack-tap-bang” drills, if a round fails to fire. In the appendix carry position the Glock produces more irritation if you are not trim, with a more portly physique the smooth wood grips of the impeccable Craig Spegal grips are very comfortable on the underside of your spare tire. So this leads us to the question is the J-Frame better? Hardly, it fits a niche in the self defense pistol pantheon of convenience and easy of use and carry, but it is not a a formidable weapon system by any stretch of the imagination. It is better than the larger more effective firearm left at home due to it’s size and weight. In closing, the Glock 26/27 is an awesome balance between size, weight and firepower, but it is not a replacement for the venerable J-Frame, that has it’s own role to play in your stable of personal protection firearms.
