Reloading for the J-Frame (cheaply and easily)

07-06-2013
#firearms

This article is not about how to stick cartridges in the chambers, but how to reload the cases with a new primer, powder and topping it with a new projectile. Reloading a .38 Special which is a straight walled, rimmed case could not be any easier. It was designed in 1898 to shoot a 158gr round nose bullet moving at about 850fps from a 6 inch barrel using 21 grains of FFg black powder. Pressure is kept to the minimal pressure of only ~20,000psi (in +p) compared to 9mm +p which runs at 38,500psi, because of it’s black powder heritage. Since, modern propellents are so much more size efficient, you will be filling only a small percentage of the case, so you must be careful not to double or triple charge the case which could have the undesired result of catastrophic failure. Lead cast bullets and the 38 Special cartridge go together like the horse and carriage. With such low pressure and slow speed; leading in the barrel is minimal even with the softest lead projectiles. Case length is not as important then a rimless cartridge, since it’s the rim that prevents the cartridge from calling through the cylinder not the any ridge that the case mouth has to sit against requiring it to be a certain length. Trimming of the casts is never needed, the low pressures cause near no stretching of the cases, and the drills through cylinder of a revolver does not require the tight tolerances of the autoloading pistols with their “rimless” cartridges. Casting for the .38 Special has no special requirements. I use Lee molds with micro-grooves and Alox tumble lube. This requires no resizing, nor any messy lubing machine. Just a quick swizzle in an old CoolWhip container with a couple of drops of Alox and then set them out to dry. Once dry, they are waxy just like a .22lr bullet, but it keeps the leading in the barrel to a minimum and the cleaning of the firearm that much quicker. There are only a couple of “tricks” to loading the .38 Special. Belling the mouth enough to not scape the lead bullet is the most important, and roll crimping the bullet enough that it does not set back during recoil, allowing a possible pike in pressure are about the only things that might be different from other cartridges you have loaded for. Pistol powder selection for the .38 Special is broad indeed, but I only stock one powder, which is Winchester 231. It capable of being used to good effect in .380 ACP, 9mm, .40, .45 ACP, .357 Sig and, of course, .38 Special. I have a basic plinking load using a 125gr cast bullet that is actually a 9mm bullet (but suffers no undue accuracy issues) and a 158gr bullet that duplicates the recoil of my self defense round.
